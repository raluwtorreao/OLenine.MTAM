Culture & Society
Lenine as a cultural spokesperson for social and environmental issues
Thus, in order to exemplify Lenine’s thematic approach in his songs, some tracks from his main albums and their respective analyses are presented below.

In Carbono, 2015 – Quede Água, the lyrics denounce the water and climate crisis in Brazil and worldwide, showing that drought already affects urban centers and is no longer a distant problem. The lyrical voice criticizes the predatory economic model, deforestation, fossil fuels, and the commodification of water, which increase inequalities and threaten life. At the same time, it proposes learning from Northeastern communities and Indigenous peoples, defending an ethical relationship with the Earth, seen as greater than humankind itself.

In Falange Canibal, 2003 – Ecos do Ão, the lyrics portray a Brazil marked by violence, poverty, and inequality, symbolized by the rebellion at FEBEM and by social “chaos.” They question corruption, the unequal distribution of wealth, and the collective feeling of impotence. Despite the critical and anguished tone, the text rejects conformism: it affirms that the country is not only pain and perversity, but also talent, invention, and cultural richness. It defends transforming dreams into concrete projects, proposing the conscious construction of a new Brazilian civilization—more just and luminous.

In Na Pressão, 1999 – Relampiano, the lyrics portray the harsh daily life of a child living in poverty, forced to work at traffic lights while the family faces abandonment, precariousness, and minimal survival. The repetitive use of common scenes reinforces the normalization of social inequality. Linguistically, the term “relampiano,” a popular and oral form instead of “relampiando,” stands out, bringing the narrative closer to everyday speech and the depicted universe. The term intensifies social realism, reinforcing the urgency, instability, and vulnerability of “Neném’s” life amid the city’s unequal growth.

In Labiata, 2008 – A Mancha, an environmental tragedy caused by an oil spill on the Brazilian coast is described in a poetic yet forceful way. The “stain” symbolizes both pollution and collective shame, spreading over rivers, fish, birds, and coastal life. The image of the pregnant dark-haired woman, daughter of a fisherman, reinforces the human and social impact of the disaster, associating environmental destruction with pain, waiting, and the interruption of possible futures, denouncing negligence and the vulnerability of traditional communities in the face of degradation.

In Lenine Incité, 2005 – Vivo, there is a reflection on the fragility and impermanence of human existence, describing the living being as incomplete, fallible, and in constant transformation. By listing physical, social, and collective threats—diseases, violence, addictions, and the evils of the modern world—the text acknowledges the harshness of contemporary life. Even so, it emphatically affirms the value of existence: being alive, even imperfect and unfinished, is presented as an act of resistance and affirmation in the face of chaos, celebrating life in its provisional condition.
